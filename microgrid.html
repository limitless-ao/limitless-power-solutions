Community Initiative

Community-Based Microgrids for Nigeria.

A decentralized, solar-led microgrid program that brings reliable electricity to Nigerian communities, replaces diesel generators, and builds local technical capacity. Designed to meet the standards of the ZE-Gen accelerator and similar climate-innovation funds.

Rationale

Why community microgrids — and why now.

Across Nigeria, millions of people and small businesses rely on diesel generators for daily power. The fuel is expensive, the emissions are harmful, and grid extension is slow. A decentralized renewable microgrid is faster, cheaper over its lifetime, and cleaner.

Community-based microgrids offer four compounding benefits:

  • ✓ Reliable, low-carbon power for residents and businesses
  • ✓ Energy sharing across households, shops and clinics
  • ✓ Storage-backed continuity through cloudy periods
  • ✓ Local jobs in installation, operation and maintenance
Working name

SustainGrid / RenewXChange

A pilot-then-replicate program: prove the model in one community, codify the playbook, then deploy across the south-west and beyond.

Methodology

A nine-step contextual playbook.

Designed for the realities of Nigerian communities — regulatory, technical, financial and social.

1

Energy needs assessment

Population, economic activity and existing infrastructure mapped per community.

2

Resource assessment

Solar irradiance, sunlight hours and load potential evaluated for each site.

3

Community engagement

Early dialogue with residents, leaders and businesses to align expectations.

4

Regulatory & policy review

Compliance with NERC, state policies and renewable-energy incentives.

5

Technology feasibility

Topology, scalability and maintenance requirements assessed per site.

6

Financial modeling

Capex, opex, tariffs, payback and funding mix — grants, equity, debt.

7

Local capacity building

Training programs that create local jobs and ensure long-term sustainability.

8

Pilot & iteration

Start with a pilot community, measure, refine, then replicate.

9

Monitoring & evaluation

Continuous tracking of impact on community, environment and energy access.

Illustrative scenario

Bringing light to a rural village.

An illustrative pilot designed to show what a community microgrid deployment looks like in practice.

  1. 1
    Community engagement. Conversations with leaders and residents about energy needs, willingness to participate, and expectations.
  2. 2
    Resource assessment. Sunlight hours and intensity confirm solar viability.
  3. 3
    Pilot installation. Solar microgrid powers a community center, school and a few homes — phones charge, lights work, children study after dark.
  4. 4
    Local operation. Trained technicians from the community manage the system; a local committee governs distribution.
  5. 5
    Expansion. Coverage extends to the full village; partnerships with local government and businesses fund expansion.
  6. 6
    Mobile payments. Phone-based tariff collection ensures sustainable revenue for maintenance and growth.
  7. 7
    Replication. Neighboring villages adopt the model; a network of community-driven microgrids takes shape.
